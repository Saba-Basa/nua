---------------------------------------------
# Step 2: Define
Write both the formal and intuitive meanings of all core terms.
---------------------------------------------

intrinsic dimension
I1:
part and parcel of the system. It tells how many independent directions belong to the system itself.
one for a line, two for a plane, three for a volume.

F1:
Let M ⊂ ℝᵈ be a smooth manifold of dimension m.
Then the intrinsic dimension of M is m; it is the number of independent coordinates
needed to describe points on M. M is a subset of ℝᵈ.

---------------------------------------------
### Core objects
---------------------------------------------

## manifold M ⊂ ℝᵈ
I1:
Any shape, for example a torus, sits in ℝ³. Every point on the torus surface needs
two numbers to locate it: one angle around the ring, one angle around the tube.
That's why it's a 2D manifold. Even though the torus lives in 3D space, you only
move in two independent directions when you stay on the surface.

Same with a sheet of paper. You need two distances to find any spot on the sheet.
If you bend the paper through space, the points still only need two coordinates
to describe their position on the sheet itself.

Locally, both surfaces are flat. Globally, they can be curved or bent through the
larger space.

F1:
A smooth manifold of dimension m is a set M such that every point x ∈ M
has a neighborhood U ⊂ M and a map φ : U → ℝᵐ that is a homeomorphism onto its image.
The collection of such pairs (U, φ) is called an atlas.
If all transition maps φ_j ∘ φ_i⁻¹ between overlapping charts are smooth,
then M is a smooth (differentiable) manifold.

## Point on the manifold  x ∈ M

I1 (Intuitive meaning):
A point on a manifold is a single position on the shape.
It has no size, no area, no volume; it is purely a location.
Even if the manifold is embedded in a higher-dimensional ambient space,
each point still lies on the manifold, not elsewhere in that space.
The ambient space may give global coordinates, but the manifold itself
restricts which positions are allowed.
A point is simply one such allowed location on the shape.

F1 (Formal meaning):
A point is an element of the set M.


------------------------------------------------------------
Q1 — What object contains the thing I am defining?
------------------------------------------------------------

A topology is a collection of subsets of a set; it cannot exist without the set.
A function is defined as a set of ordered pairs (x, y); it requires a set.
All spaces in mathematics begin as:  set  +  extra structure.
You cannot have the extra structure without the underlying set.

Coordinates come from charts,
charts come from functions,
functions come from sets.

Conceptual layers:
Layer 1: Set                (points exist)
Layer 2: Topology           (neighborhoods exist)
Layer 3: Differentiability  (charts exist)
Layer 4: Metric             (distances exist)
Layer 5: Curvature          (bending exists)

A point sits at Layer 1.
Therefore Q1 chooses:  the set M.

Why must the point belong to the lowest layer?
A point needs no structure at all:
not topology,
not differentiability,
not metric,
not coordinates.

All a point needs is a collection it belongs to.

A set is the minimal mathematical structure that can contain objects.
A manifold, topology, metric, or chart cannot exist without first having a set.

Therefore, the primitive identity of a point is:
an element of the set M.
